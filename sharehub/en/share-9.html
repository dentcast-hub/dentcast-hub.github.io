Share Hub — Smoking and Early Dental Implant Failure

فارسی

Smoking is one of the most important modifiable risk factors in implant therapy. In this episode, Dr. Narges Shojaei (periodontist) discusses the effect of smoking on dental implants and oral surgery; from the biological mechanisms of failure to practical protocols for managing the smoking patient.

∆ Before Any Clinical Event: The Effect of Smoke on the Implant Surface

A point that is often overlooked is that cigarette smoke affects the implant surface itself and its wettability before any clinical event, and this very thing disrupts the first stage of osseointegration. In other words, the damage begins at the very first contact of the implant surface with the environment, not merely in late complications.

∆ The Biological Mechanisms of Failure

  • Hypoxia: reduced tissue oxygen delivery that slows healing and bone formation.
  • Nicotine: an effect on local circulation and the activity of bone-forming cells.
  • Disruption of the balance of bone remodeling and the immune response: a setting that drives the progression of peri-implantitis and resorption of the tissue around the implant.

∆ The Clinical Picture in the Smoking Patient

  • The risk of failure in smokers is meaningfully higher than in non-smokers.
  • The maxilla, owing to its bone quality, is more vulnerable, and failure is seen there more frequently.
  • Quitting or reducing smoking is genuinely effective only when it is done within the appropriate time window before and after surgery, not merely a momentary cessation on the day of the procedure.

∆ Practical Considerations in Patient Management

  • Implant surface selection appropriate to the smoking patient's condition.
  • Attention to surgical technique and reduction of tissue trauma.
  • Adjusting follow-up intervals and regular monitoring of marginal bone loss in these patients.

∆ Beyond Cigarettes: Hookah, Vaping, and Cannabis

The final part turns to substances that are less studied in this context: hookah, vaping and e-cigarettes, and cannabis. The message is clear: none of them is a safe alternative to cigarettes, and they should not be presented to the patient as a "less harmful" option.

∆ Summary

If you work with implants or have smoking patients, this episode both clarifies the mechanism and gives you a practical framework for decision-making.
